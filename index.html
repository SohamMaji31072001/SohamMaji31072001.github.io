Introduction

Galactic dynamo theory is a conceptual framework aimed at explaining the origin and maintenance of magnetic fields observed in galaxies, including our own Milky Way. It proposes that these magnetic fields are generated and sustained through the dynamo process, which involves the conversion of kinetic energy from galactic rotation and turbulent motions into magnetic energy. The dynamo process operates through the combined effects of differential rotation and turbulence within the interstellar medium of galaxies. As the galaxy rotates differentially, with regions near the center rotating faster than those farther out, it generates shear motions that stretch and amplify existing magnetic fields. Turbulence, caused by various astrophysical processes such as supernova explosions and stellar winds, further enhances the amplification of magnetic fields by stirring and mixing the interstellar gas. The amplification of magnetic fields through the dynamo process results in the formation of large-scale, coherent magnetic structures that permeate the entire galaxy. These magnetic fields play crucial roles in various astrophysical processes, such as shaping the dynamics of interstellar gas, regulating star formation, and influencing the propagation of cosmic rays. Galactic dynamo theory continues to be an active area of research in astrophysics, with ongoing efforts to refine and validate theoretical models through observations and numerical simulations. By better understanding the mechanisms driving the generation and evolution of galactic magnetic fields, scientists aim to unravel the complex interplay between magnetic fields and the broader astrophysical phenomena occurring within galaxies.
